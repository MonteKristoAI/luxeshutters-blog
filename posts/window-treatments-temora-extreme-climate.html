The Week We Measured 41°C on a Saturday and 3°C on a Tuesday

The Riverina is not a "warm climate" or a "cold climate". It is both, often inside the same fortnight, and the standard window treatment advice you find online assumes you only have to solve one of the two. According to historical Bureau of Meteorology data summarised on the Temora climate record, the town has hit a high of 46.4°C (4 January 2020) and a low of -6.8°C (29 August 2018). Mean January maximum sits at 31.5°C; mean July minimum at 2.1°C. So your windows have to handle a 53-degree swing across the calendar.

That swing matters because windows are the weakest thermal point in almost every Australian home. We see the same construction over and over: single-pane glazing, aluminium frames, no thermal break. On the day a homeowner in Cootamundra rings to ask why one room is "always too hot or too cold", the answer is almost always the glass. The covering across that glass is the cheapest and fastest way to fix it, well before anyone goes near the air conditioning bill.

Temora also has a wide diurnal range. A 38°C summer afternoon can drop into the mid-teens by 4am. So a covering that is "set and forget" in a temperate Sydney terrace simply does not fit here. You need adjustability, and you need it on the openings that take the worst of the sun and the worst of the cold.

Why Window Treatments Temora Extreme Heat Cold Homes Need Are Different

Up to 87% of summer heat gain in an Australian home enters through the windows on hot days, and on cold days windows account for 40% of heat loss, per research summarised in The Conversation citing the Australian Government's YourHome guide. Heating and cooling already represent roughly 40% of an Australian household's energy use. So the glass area in a typical Riverina lounge is doing more to set the power bill than the air conditioner ever will.

Heat behaves predictably through that glass. On a 40-plus day in Griffith, sunlight strikes the pane and radiant energy pours straight into the room, regardless of how cool the outside air feels in shade. On a frosty Junee morning, warm room air contacts the cold glass, drops in temperature, slides down the wall and pools at floor level. Both effects are dramatic, and both happen in homes built to perfectly reasonable Australian standards.

Older Riverina homes carry an extra burden. Federation cottages around Temora, weatherboard farmhouses out toward West Wyalong, and the brick-veneer builds that went up across Wagga in the 1980s and 90s all share the same vulnerability: tall, single-glazed sash and casement windows with very little around them to slow heat transfer. We have walked through dozens of these places where curtains stop short of the floor and sit on a flimsy track with no pelmet. From a thermal point of view, that arrangement is barely there.

Window Treatments Temora Extreme Heat Cold Tradeoffs: The Summer Side

The honest truth, drawn from a peer-reviewed study by Newcastle Innovation (Alterman and O'Keeffe, 2016) on Australian conditions, is that where you place the covering matters more than what it is made of. External products beat internal products on summer heat reduction every time. The same study found a white external aluminium plantation shutter cut heat entering the room by 38.7%, while a white internal plantation shutter sat between 26.5% and 32.1% depending on the eco frame configuration.

For a Temora home with serious west-facing exposure, our usual recommendation pairs an external product with an internal one. We fit a CWGlobal Atlas aluminium shutter or a zipscreen (an external aluminium-framed mesh blind that locks into side tracks, blocking radiant heat before it reaches the glass) on the outside of the worst-affected windows, then run an internal CWGlobal Forte Select honeycomb blind or plantation shutter inside as the final layer. The external product stops the radiant heat at the glass; the internal product handles glare control, privacy, and the residual conduction.

Colour is the second variable people underrate. The same Newcastle Innovation study showed that switching a plantation shutter from white to black drops the heat reduction from over 26% to as low as 10.1%, because dark surfaces absorb radiant energy and re-emit it into the room. A black shutter looks beautiful in a south-facing study; on a west-facing lounge in Temora it is a thermal liability. We tell every homeowner the same thing: keep the colour light on the heat-loaded elevations, and save the dark stains for shaded interior rooms.

Window Treatments Temora Extreme Heat Cold Tradeoffs: The Frost Side

Cold-side performance is a different game. Once the room air starts dropping at night, the priority shifts from blocking radiation to trapping a still-air layer right against the glass. That is where pelmets, honeycomb cells, and snug-fitting plantation louvres earn their keep. YourHome's glazing guide is explicit on this point: snugly fitted blinds and curtains with pelmets reduce convective heat transfer by trapping a layer of still air, and the gaps around the perimeter are what destroy that performance.

For a Wagga Wagga client we fitted last winter, the change was straightforward. We replaced a thin sheer curtain on a flimsy hooks-and-track system with a plantation shutter (Bayview ThermoPoly, white), then ran a heavy lined Aura Drapery curtain over the top with a proper closed pelmet returning to the wall. Surface temperature on the inside of the room at 6am went from a measured 11°C on the cold morning before the install to 17°C after, with the same heating settings. That is the still-air layer doing its job.

Honeycomb (cellular) blinds deserve their own mention. Honeycomb blinds are pleated fabric blinds with hexagonal air pockets running across the width of each cell, and a quality double-cell product noticeably slows conduction through the glass. We use them most often in bedrooms and along south-facing elevations where summer heat is mild and winter cold is the bigger problem. They are also the lightest-touch option for renters, since they fit inside the architrave with brackets that screw into the timber.

Mistakes Riverina Homeowners Make (and One We Used to Make)

Three patterns come up again and again on quoting visits, so we list them honestly.

The first is the "cheapest curtain on the longest track" trap. A flimsy white sheer hung on a 1980s ceiling track does almost nothing for thermal performance. People assume any curtain helps. It barely does. Without a pelmet to seal the top, warm air slips behind the curtain, hits the cold glass, drops, and circulates the room as a draft. We see this set-up every week.

The second is choosing colour for a magazine, not a climate. We have lost count of how many west-facing lounges we have walked into where dark grey or black timber-look shutters are absolutely cooking the room because the homeowner wanted the Pinterest aesthetic. The shutters are beautiful. They are also working against the air conditioning every January afternoon.

The third is one we used to get wrong ourselves. Early on, we pushed plantation shutters as a standalone cold-weather answer in Riverina farmhouses with single glazing. They are excellent, but a shutter on its own does not seal the top of the window cavity. We now recommend a curtain with a pelmet over the top wherever winter performance matters. It costs more. It works better. We learnt the lesson the slow way, watching too many homeowners with beautiful shutters still complain about cold rooms in July.

Frequently Asked Questions

What window treatments handle Temora extreme heat cold conditions best?

External shutters and zipscreens handle the heat side, while internal plantation shutters paired with a heavy lined curtain and pelmet handle the cold. Newcastle Innovation testing found a white external aluminium plantation shutter cuts heat into the room by 38.7%, white internal plantation shutters by 26.5% to 32.1%, and a block-out roller blind by 18.3% (Alterman and O'Keeffe, 2016). Pair an internal product with a pelmet and you trap the still-air gap that stops cold air dropping off the glass at 5am.

Are plantation shutters good for Riverina frost mornings?

Yes, when fitted snug to the architrave with no gap at the head or sill. PVC or ThermoPoly louvres trap a layer of still air against the glass, which is what stops the cold draft from sliding into the room. We see Bayview ThermoPoly hold up best in homes near Temora and Junee where the mercury drops below zero on still mornings. Add a pelmet over the curtain track and you have two stages of insulation working together at 5am, which is when the cold complaint always comes in.

Do honeycomb blinds work in extreme Australian heat?

They work well as the inside layer because the cellular pockets trap air and slow heat transfer through glass. They are not the front-line defence against 40-degree summer sun on a west-facing window. The smarter setup in a home like ours pairs a honeycomb blind on the inside with an external Atlas zipscreen or aluminium shutter that catches the heat before it ever reaches the glazing. The Newcastle Innovation study confirmed external products consistently outperform internal-only setups for summer heat reduction.

How much can good window treatments Temora extreme heat cold loads actually save on a Riverina power bill?

Heating and cooling typically account for around 40% of a household's energy use, and up to 87% of summer heat gain comes through the windows on hot days according to YourHome (the Australian Government's residential energy resource). Trim that gain with the right product and the cooling load drops sharply. We have had Wagga homeowners report 20% to 30% lower summer power bills after fitting external Atlas shutters or zipscreens to north and west elevations, alongside their existing ducted system.
