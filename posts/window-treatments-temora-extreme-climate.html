What "Extreme" Actually Means in Temora's Climate

Before recommending product, it helps to be specific about what your window furnishings have to survive. According to Bureau of Meteorology data for Temora (station 073038, 1934 to 2005), the mean January maximum sits at 31.5 degrees, with summer days regularly pushing into the high thirties. The mean July minimum is 2.1 degrees, which means a meaningful share of winter mornings sit below freezing. Annual rainfall is a modest 525.7mm, much of it falling outside the heating season, so dry frost is the dominant winter problem rather than damp.

That climate punishes window furnishings in three specific ways. First, PVC and unsealed timber shutters cop direct UV through hot afternoons, especially on west-facing openings. Second, lightweight fabric blinds and curtains lose their thermal grip overnight when the temperature crashes. Third, anything fabric-only with no pelmet creates a cold-air convection loop, where chilled air drops down behind the curtain and warm room air rises to meet it, then dumps heat onto the glass.

For example, if you live in a heritage federation home in Junee or a 1990s brick veneer in Wagga, the same physics applies. Your window treatments are the second line of defence after your glazing itself, and in most older Riverina homes the glass is single-pane. So the coverings carry a heavier load than they would in a modern double-glazed Sydney unit.

Window Treatments for Temora's Extreme Heat: What Works in Summer

For summer, the rule we live by is simple: stop the sun before it hits the glass. Australian government guidance from energy.gov.au notes that almost 90 percent of unwanted summer heat enters a home through windows, and external shading is by a wide margin the most effective way to block it (2025). Once the sun is through the glass, an internal blind can only reflect a portion of the radiation that has already become heat inside your room.

So we fit aluminium plantation shutters from the Atlas range, or external zipscreens, on the worst-affected openings of Riverina homes. In practice, aluminium plantation shutters can reduce solar heat gain by up to 90 percent when fully closed, with the louvre angle giving you fine control over light and ventilation. On west-facing openings in Temora and Griffith, for instance, external coverings often pay for themselves through cooling savings within five summers.

Inside the home, the next best summer performer is a properly sealed cellular honeycomb blind. In fact, tightly installed cellular shades can reduce unwanted solar heat gain through windows by up to 60 percent (Window Shading Association of Australia, 2025), thanks to the trapped air pocket inside each cell. They are particularly effective on south and east-facing windows where you still want soft daylight. For our Riverina clients with budget constraints, we often pair a single external aluminium blind on the worst west-facing opening with internal cellular blinds elsewhere. As a result, you capture most of the summer benefit at a fraction of full external coverage.

Window Treatments for Temora's Extreme Cold: What Works on Frosty Nights

Winter performance is a different game from the summer side. Now you want to stop heat escaping rather than stop heat entering. According to yourhome.gov.au, up to 40 percent of a home's heating energy is lost through unfurnished windows. Sustainability Victoria notes that a single pane of glass can lose almost ten times as much heat as the same area of insulated wall (Sustainability Victoria, 2025). On a 2 degree Temora morning, that gap is the difference between a comfortable lounge and one you can see your breath in.

Three winners on the cold side, in order. First, double-cell honeycomb blinds inside a sealed pelmet box. The double-cell construction adds two trapped air layers, and on top of that the pelmet stops the cold-air convection loop we mentioned earlier. For example, the Window Shading Association of Australia cites Glasgow Caledonian University research showing a reflective roller blind alone can cut heat loss by 40 percent compared with an unfurnished single-glazed window (WSAA, 2025). In practice a double-cell honeycomb in a pelmet box outperforms that figure.

Second, blockout curtains with a sealed pelmet and floor-length drop. Heavy-weight blockout drapery in the Aura range, hung against the wall with a pelmet on top and side returns, traps a deep air pocket between the fabric and the glass. The pelmet is the unsung hero here. Without one, ordinary curtains barely beat 10 percent heat-loss reduction, because warm room air still loops over the top.

Third, plantation shutters in Bayview ThermoPoly or basswood timber. Solid panels create a still-air gap against the glass, and ThermoPoly has the added benefit of not warping in humid Riverina mornings when condensation forms on the cold glass overnight. Although shutters are not as thermally aggressive as a sealed cellular blind, they last 25 years, look smart, and add resale value. As a result, most clients reckon they even up the maths over time.

The Layered Window Treatments Temora Extreme Heat Cold Homes Actually Need

For most Temora and surrounding clients, no single product handles both the 31 degree summer afternoons and the 2 degree winter dawns equally well. So the honest answer is layering. Our standard recommendation for west-facing living rooms in the Riverina runs something like this: external aluminium blinds or zipscreens to stop summer sun at the glass, plus internal plantation shutters for daytime ventilation control and resale value, plus pelmeted blockout curtains for winter overnight insulation. That sounds like overkill until you cost out a single new air-conditioner replacement after a heat-related compressor failure, or a $200 monthly shock on a winter gas bill.

By contrast, for south and east-facing windows where summer sun is less aggressive, double-cell honeycomb blinds in a pelmet box do most of the work in a single product. For these openings we use the CWGlobal Forte Select cellular range, which is Australian-manufactured and rated under the Window Energy Rating Scheme (WERS, 2025). Pair them with a simple sheer in front for daytime privacy, and the room behaves well year-round.

For bedrooms, especially children's rooms, we instead lean toward Bayview ThermoPoly plantation shutters as the primary cover. They are scratch-tolerant, washable, and the louvre control means a child can wake to soft morning light or sleep through a 7am summer sunrise. In addition we add a blockout roller behind the shutter for the rare hot night where you want total light control without sacrificing the shutter's thermal layer.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best window treatment for Temora's hot summer afternoons?

External aluminium blinds or zipscreens are the best single performer for hot summers in Temora and the wider Riverina, because they stop solar radiation before it touches the glass. Internal options that work well include double-cell honeycomb blinds and aluminium plantation shutters, which can cut radiant heat gain by up to 60 percent (WSAA, 2025). On west-facing rooms the layered approach (external blind plus internal shutter) consistently beats any single product, particularly during the long stretches in January and February when afternoons regularly push above 35 degrees in Temora.

Are honeycomb blinds worth it on a frosty Riverina morning?

Yes, when fitted properly. A double-cell honeycomb blind in a sealed pelmet box can reduce overnight heat loss by up to 60 percent compared with a bare single-glazed window. The trapped air inside the cells acts like wall insulation, and the pelmet stops the cold-air convection loop that ruins the performance of fabric coverings hung loose on a rod. We have fitted these in homes from Junee to Cootamundra, and they consistently deliver the noticeable warmth lift that homeowners are after on sub-zero mornings.

Do plantation shutters really insulate, or is that marketing?

They insulate, but less aggressively than a sealed cellular blind. A timber or ThermoPoly plantation shutter creates a still-air gap against the glass, cutting heat loss meaningfully but not by the 40 to 60 percent that a properly pelmeted cellular achieves. Where shutters earn their keep is durability, ventilation control through the louvres, and resale value. Most of our clients fit shutters as a 25-year decision, then layer a curtain or blind for the deepest winter nights. The Window Shading Association classifies louvred panels as "fine furniture rather than a disposable accessory" because of that long working life.

What is the cheapest way to handle Temora's extreme heat and cold without a full retrofit?

If budget is tight, the single move that earns you the most warmth per dollar is fitting a sealed pelmet to your existing curtains and adding a low-cost cellular blind behind them. The pelmet alone shifts heavy curtains from about 10 percent heat-loss reduction to closer to 25 percent. Adding a single cellular blind behind brings that into the 40 to 50 percent range, which is enough to make a Temora 2 degree morning feel materially different in the lounge. We have done this kind of staged retrofit for retirees in Wagga and Young who could not justify a full window furnishing replacement.
