What Pushes the Price Up or Brings It Down?

Every dwelling is different. Over years of quoting across the Riverina, we've encountered everything from straightforward rectangles to elaborate heritage bays that consumed half a day just to template. So what actually nudges your louvre investment higher or lower? These variables explain most of the variance.

Factors That Increase Cost

  • Non-standard shapes: Arched, circular, or raked openings demand bespoke frames and cardboard templates. Consequently, expect 20-40% above the equivalent rectangular area.
  • Bay configurations: Three or more angled panels wrapping around a bay add significant complexity. Chris templates the precise angles on site because even two degrees of misalignment ruins the fit.
  • Sliding or bi-fold track systems: Rail-mounted assemblies for glass sliders carry higher hardware expenses than standard hinged panels.
  • Stained timber finishes: If you'd prefer the natural grain visible rather than painted white, basswood stained adds roughly $80-$120 per m2 on top.
  • Elevated or restricted-access openings: If scaffolding or specialised equipment is required, fitting naturally takes longer and raises labour charges.

Factors That Save You Money

  • Whole-home orders: Completing 8-12 openings in a single visit is far more efficient than scheduling three separate trips. As a result, we typically discount 10-15% off the per-opening rate for bulk jobs.
  • Polymer over timber: Honestly? For white-painted louvres, most homeowners can't distinguish PVC from basswood at arm's length. Choosing polymer therefore saves 30-40% with zero visible compromise.
  • Standard rectangular openings: Straightforward geometry means faster fitting and lower labour. It really is that simple.
  • Recess-fit mounting: Seating panels inside the reveal is quicker than face-mounting with an external architrave surround. So if your openings allow it, recess-fit trims both time and expense.

Which Material Should You Choose for Each Room?

Laboratory testing demonstrates that louvred panels slash heat flux through glazing by 20-58%, depending on the cavity depth and substrate composition (Luxaflex, 2026). That's a tangible difference on your quarterly electricity statement. However, which substrate belongs in which space?

Having fitted louvres in virtually every dwelling type across the Riverina, from century-old weatherboards in Temora to fresh builds on Wagga's suburban fringe, here's our room-by-room verdict.

PVC (Forte Select, from $300/m2): Easily our highest-volume product. It resists moisture, withstands scorching temperatures, wipes clean with a damp cloth, and in a white lacquer finish it's visually identical to natural timber. If you're outfitting an entire residence on a sensible budget, this is where most Riverina families gravitate. It excels in bathrooms, kitchens, laundries, and children's bedrooms where resilience trumps character grain.

ThermoPoly (Bayview ThermoPoly, from $350/m2): Specifically formulated for harsh Australian conditions, this composite sits a notch above standard polymer in both thermal resistance and panel rigidity. Because Riverina afternoons frequently surpass 40 degrees while winter dawns bring genuine frost, that performance differential is tangible rather than theoretical. We especially advocate ThermoPoly for westerly-exposed openings that absorb relentless afternoon radiation.

Basswood (Bayview Basswood, from $500/m2): Genuine hardwood carrying a tactile warmth and heft that engineered alternatives can't entirely replicate. Lighter than polymer yet sturdier than softwood, basswood reveals a beautiful natural figuring in stained variants. It's the preferred selection for lounges and master suites in heritage dwellings across Temora and Wagga, where occupants prioritise a premium aesthetic. Just avoid deploying it in splash-prone zones.

Here's the nuance most comparison articles overlook. Are Cheap Imported Shutters Worth Considering?

Inevitably, some homeowners discover offshore alternatives advertised at $150-$200 per square metre and wonder whether the plantation shutters cost premium for Australian-made products is justified. It's a fair question, so here's what we've observed first-hand.

First, imported panels frequently arrive with dimensional tolerances wider than domestic specifications permit. Even a 2mm discrepancy across a 1200mm span creates visible gaps between louvre and frame. Second, paint adhesion on budget composites tends to deteriorate within 18-24 months, particularly in UV-intense regions like central NSW. Third, warranty enforcement becomes practically impossible when the manufacturer operates from a different jurisdiction.

By contrast, CWGlobal products are fabricated domestically under Australian Standard AS2663.2, verified through the WSAA certification programme, and backed by enforceable local guarantees. Does that justify paying more upfront? In our professional judgement, unequivocally yes. The customers who've asked us to replace failing imported panels after barely two years would almost certainly agree.

Ultimately, the plantation shutters cost discussion isn't purely about the sticker figure on day one. It's about total expenditure across the product's operational life. A $500/m2 panel that performs flawlessly for 25 years works out cheaper per annum than a $200/m2 import that needs replacing at year three.
